§ The Premise
Training is not telling. It is the deliberate transfer of capacity — anchored to an objective, measured in behaviour, proven on Monday morning.
Skills Objectives Transfer Unleash Potential Dialogue by Design Measured by Change
The Honest Test If nothing is different on Monday morning, the session was theatre. Every framework ahead is the rehearsal that prevents this. Most of what is called training is, in fact, presentation — a trainer stands in front of a room and makes information available. Information is then mistaken for transfer; attendance for learning; applause for change. A trainer who knows the difference can begin the actual work.

02
Two Philosophies of Teaching
Andragogy · Pedagogy · Six Dimensions

The term andragogy comes from the Greek aner / andros — meaning "adult" — coined by Alexander Kapp in 1833 and popularised by Malcolm Knowles in 1968. Its counterpart, pedagogy, has its roots in the teaching of children. The two are not merely different in tone; they differ on six fundamental dimensions.

Kapp 1833 · Knowles 1968
Dimension
Andragogy · Adults
Pedagogy · Children
Driver
Their own experience and goals. Adults want to know why before how.
External authority. The teacher decides what is worth knowing.
Self-Concept
Self-directed. Treats imposed instruction as condescension.
Dependent on the teacher to structure and pace the learning.
Experience
Experience is a resource — the curriculum is partly inside the room.
Limited experience; the teacher supplies most of the raw material.
Readiness
Triggered by life circumstance — a job, a deadline, a real problem.
Sequenced by curriculum and developmental stage.
Orientation
Problem-centred. They want tools that work on Monday.
Subject-centred. Knowledge organised by discipline.
Motivation
Internal — relevance, mastery, identity, advancement.
External — grades, approval, future promise.

A Caveat From Mehrabian Himself
The 7-38-55 ratio applies specifically to expressed feelings and attitudes when channels conflict. It does not mean only seven percent of all communication is verbal — use it as a teaching mnemonic that points at a real lesson: when the body and the words disagree, the body wins.

The cycle of real learning

Kolb's model argues that adults do not learn from experience alone — they learn from reflecting on experience. Every complete learner passes through four stations.

A trainer's job is to engineer each stage deliberately: stage the experience, invite reflection, supply the theory, then send the learner back to practice. Miss one station and the cycle breaks.

Honey & Mumford mapped four learning styles onto these four stages — see Section 09.

Top-Right · Logical + Extrovert

The Kirkpatrick-Phillips Ladder of Proof
Five levels · from smile-sheet to ROI

Donald Kirkpatrick gave us four levels at which training can be measured. Jack Phillips added a fifth — Return on Investment — that translates the work into the language of finance. Most failed training programmes collapse not because they were badly delivered, but because they were only ever measured at Level 1.

L1
Reaction
Did they like it? Feeling, engagement, perceived relevance, trainer style.
Smile-sheet
Live polls · NPS
L2
Learning
Did they actually learn? Knowledge and skill gain attributable to the session.
Pre-post tests
Assignments · Skill demo
L3
Behaviour
Are they doing it differently? Measured months later, on the job — where it counts.
3-6 months
Observation · Coaching
L4
Results
Did the business move? Lagging indicators tied to the original analysis.
6-12 months
Business KPIs
L5
ROI · Return on Investment
What was the financial return? Converts behaviour and business results into monetary terms — cost versus measurable gain.
Phillips extension
~2 years
◆ The Discipline
Most organisations claim Level 4. Most evaluate at Level 1. The gap between the two is where training budgets go to die. The mature trainer designs for Level 3 minimum — behaviour change on the job — and returns to measure it, honestly, months later.
§  Kirkpatrick 1959 · Phillips 1996

Voices from the Room
Recorded feedback & discussion threads · Riyadh cohort · April 2026

A two-day training is not measured by certificates issued — it is measured by the conversations it sparks, the stories participants choose to share, and the questions the cohort wrestles with together. What follows is recorded with names withheld, faithful to what was actually said in the room.

◆ Discussion Threads · The Group Sat in a Circle

Two questions that didn't have single answers

At certain moments the masterclass dissolved into open discussion — the chairs rearranged into a circle, the framework was set aside, and the cohort wrestled with questions that have no single textbook answer. These are two of those moments.

§ Discussion 01 · The Honest Question
What if a participant asks you a question — and you don't know the answer?
— Voices around the circle —
A · The cultural answer

"It depends on the culture. In some societies, saying 'I don't know' is a sign of integrity — it costs you nothing."

B · The pragmatic answer

"I'd say: 'Let me research that and come back to you.' Honest, professional, and it gives me a follow-up touchpoint."

C · The cautious answer

"In some cultures, the moment you say 'I don't know', the trainees' trust in you wavers. The room shifts. You have to read the audience."

◆ The shared insight

All three answers were right. The skill is knowing which one the room in front of you needs.

?
§ Discussion 02 · The Difficult Participant
What if you have a participant who already knows everything?
i
Engage them

Keep them busy. Give them tasks that channel their energy productively rather than letting it disrupt the room.

ii
Promote them

Give them an additional role — co-facilitator, peer mentor, breakout-group leader. Move them from competitor to ally.

iii
Earn them

Win them over. Find the gap they don't yet know about — surprise them with insight that earns their respect.

Three different verbs — engage, promote, earn — three different philosophies of conflict transformation. The cohort agreed: there is no universal right answer. The right answer is the one that turns a difficult participant into the room's strongest advocate by the end of the day.
